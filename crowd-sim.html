VR Crowd Sim is an app that allows people to practice speaking in front of a virtual audience in order to overcome stage fright. The audience murmurs to each other before the player steps on stage and begins to quiet down in response to audio picked up by the headset.

apk for Oculus Go




 The project brief

Work in a team of two to create any XR experience that is something meaningful, informative, beautiful, or all three. Try to not have the three dimensional spatial aspect of XR be an irrelevant gimmick but rather something more intrinsic where the overall user experience is delightful.





 The inspiration

Because this project was so open-ended, my partner and I first focused on things we both enjoyed. We settled on music, and from there, brainstormed ideas where a 3D space would add to an experience.We started thinking of ways music was limited in the real-world. We identified that many musicians perform in unfamiliar venues and in front of large audiences, yet many practice alone in comfortable environments.


We realized that this disparity between practice settings and performance settings leads to stage fright and anxiousness when performing.






 The problem


Though we started with the idea of improving the musicians' experiences of performing, we realized that problem we were really targeting was overcoming stage fright. Instead of building something that would be just for musicians, we envisioned how we could help anyone who needs to get up on a stage, but has stage fright.

 Our answer


Our crowd simulation app consists of an auditorium with a virtual crowd that responds to the user.





1. When the user first put on the headset, they find themselves offstage.
2. They can hear the audience murmuring and talking amongst themselves.
3. When they click on the controller, the lights dim and the user automatically walks onstage, stopping at the podium.
4. When the user begins speaking/playing music, the audience quiets down.


To simulate a real audience, the audience members shift in their seats and will start murmuring again if there is too long of a pause by the speaker/performer. Like a live audience, some audience members will cause disturbances and walk out of the auditorium.





 The details




We modelled almost all of the art assets used in the app. We also animated multiple resting animations for the audience like breathing, turning, and repositioning hands. Transitioning between these animations made movements a bit more organic as each person animated independently from the rest of the audience. We also created walking animations that had a small probability of being triggered for each person in the audience. When triggered, the person would get up from their seat and walk to the nearest exit.

We simulated the audio of the audience murmuring by listening for sound from the user. When the headset picked up sound that was above a certain level, the audience would slowly quiet down. If the headset does not pick up sound for a set amount of time, the audience begins murmuring to each other again.





 The expansions


For Speakers

To allow speakers to practice their speech, we want to add a feature where our app can connect to Google Drive where the user can display their presentation on the screen behind them. This way, they can practice more realistically in front of an audience.

For Musicians

We want to add a feature for musicians where they can upload their sheet music and it will appear on the podium/stand on stage. Having this feature allows musicians (who don't need to look at their hands when playing) to practice in front of our virtual crowd. Another beneficial addition would be to model different locations from a school auditorium to Carnegie Hall. This would allow the musician to slowly work their way up to larger audiences in more stressful performance halls.





 The challenges


The biggest challenge we faced was balancing how realistic we could make our app and how much the Oculus Go could handle. Because we had so many chair models, people models, and lighting effects, the headset became rather laggy. To fix this issue, we had to simplify all of the models and fill only about 10% of the seats with people. Because the user can't move off the stage, we used lighting to hide some of the lower resolution models in the back of the auditorium. We also had to reduce our lighting effects like lens flares that would have simulated a spotlight-type glare from the stage.

Another challenge was making the people look realistic. We worked with four pre-made human models, and tried to vary their appearances by editing materials in Photoshop. However, we soon realized that drastically different clothing colors make it easier to pinpoint which models were the same in the audience. Rather than create a new materal for each person, we kept the default ones and made subtle changes to the shades of their clothing. The other challenge was animating the people. We prevented their movements from being in sync with each other by creating multiple resting animations and using code to transition between random animations for each person.






 The takeaways


This project taught me a lot about the impacts that VR can have. This kind of technology really opens a whole new realm of use that we still have to explore. It also taught me about the limits and difficulties of working with such new products.


I really enjoyed being able to create something that is both useful and impactful. I'm also really glad that I could experiment with VR and really push the boundaries of what I could build.
