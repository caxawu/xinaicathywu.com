ARTrek is a cross-platform augmented-reality mobile indoor navigation app. The app has two sections. As a finder, the user inputs their destination and follows the AR path markers that appear. As a forger, that same user can mark a path for others to use.

 The pain points

1. As a student who has taken a couple classes in our Thayer School of Engineering, I still get lost in the building sometimes. Between the strange layout of the building that is actually comprised of two buildings and the interesting placement of auditoriums in both the basement and the second floor, the maps on the walls are, needless to say, not very helpful.

2. I was lucky enough to study abroad last year in France and travel a bit. One thing I found amazing in Switzerland was how accurate the trains were. They arrived and left exactly on the minute. When getting somewhere is confusing enough to need such an email or text, the email or text doesn't do much more to help.


Digital maps today have gotten pretty good though. If they can help someone navigate to basicially anywhere on the street, why can’t the same type of system exist for indoor destinations?

 The details


ARTrek is an augmented reality app that consists of two elements: Finder and Forger.




Finder

On the finder side, users use their phone to recognize their surroundings to launch the augmented reality path markers. Once they pop up, the user follows this path to get to their location.


To be a finder, users:


1. Click on finder in the home screen.
2. Type in the place/room they are looking for.
3. Scan their surroundings so the app knows where they are in the building.
4. Follow the AR path markers that appear to their destination!


Forger

On the forger side, users who are familiar with a location can place path markers to help others. Existing digital maps rely on premapping all existing streets and roads so that it can suggest paths to users. This process is not feasible for mapping every building. So, we used a "Waze-like" approach where users can contribute to a network of defined paths. By making the data user-driven, more buildings can be mapped—allowing more paths become available for people to use.


To be a forger, users:


1. Click on forger in the home screen.
2. Type in your current (starting) location.
3. Scan the surroundings so the app know where they are in the building.
4. Place the AR path markers as they walk to the end destination.
5. Submit their path to the database of paths on Microsoft's Azure Cloud.


To maintain the accuracy and reliability of the paths, a submitted path would only be verified as an offical path once enough people have marked the same path to a destination. To maintain the reliability of the system, a path is only verified and uploaded for all users to see after multiple people have marked the same path to a destination.





 The bonus features


Though we focused our user group on people navigating a confusing building, our solution also targets the other pain points we identified.

Personalized Paths

Because uses can contribute paths anyways, being able to send a specific path to a specific person could comes in handy. This could be used to show a friend/delivery person how to get from the front of an apartment complex to a specific door since following a path on a phone is much simplier than trying to decipher a message specifying the steps. This also allows helps people to meet up more easily in hard-to-find/less-travelled locations.

Quick Updating

User-driven data also allows paths to be updated quickly. In the face of construction in front of someone's house, or a last-minute room change, this feature is really helpful. As long as one person maps the path, everyone else can easily follow it around the construction/to the right room.





 The challenges


We ran into a lot of version incompatibilities between ARCore, Android Studio, and Unity. A good portion of the time was dedicated to redownloaded different versions to try to get everything to connect. My brother and I also had to learn how to use ARCore to recognize a location instead of a still image. We also had to find a way to save this path information. Luckily, with the help of the Microsoft reps at the event, we found that Microsoft's Azure Cloud Anchors provided exactly what we needed. The software also provided example code of how these anchors worked. We spent a lot of time looking through the code, and ultimately modifying it to build our project.
