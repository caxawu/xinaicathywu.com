DartTree

Skills: User Research, Interviewing, Needfinding, Prototyping, Iterating

Paper

The prompt

Use the design process to enable students to prepare for and pursue careers "off the beaten path."

The research

To tackle this challenge my team first set out to understand what "off the beaten path" careers were. In our research, we found Dartmouth's 2018 Cap & Gown Survey.
From this data that this survey provided, it became rather clear what the distribution of career paths were. In the words of Mindy Kaling:



But, besides those going into finance, a substantial number of students are also entering the tech sector. So, we focused on the 37% of students who fell under the "other category." We defined this group as the "off-pathers" and focused on them as our user group.

The problem

Because so many Dartmouth graduates go into either finance and tech, many resources exist for them. They have excellent support from the Center for Professional Development (CPD) on campus and a strong alumni network. However, "off-pathers" have a lack of support and have to navigate the professional world by themselves. To enable students off-the-beaten-path to better prepare for and pursue their careers, they need to be able to have greater exposure to their field of interest.

Our answer

When we synthesized information from interviewing students pursuing off-the-beaten-path careers, we found that this group found alumni to be the most helpful. So, we focused on improving this connection. Students needed easier means of connecting with alumni who can help develop their interests and provide guidance in their professional pursuits. Specifically, we focused on increasing accessibility and decreasing intimidation in regard to reaching out to alumni.

Our proposed solution is DartTree—an interest-based networking platform that connects alumni and students through information that cannot be found on LinkedIn or the Dartmouth Alumni Career Network. DartTree is a redesign of the Dartmouth Alumni Career Network (ACN). Because it is interest-based, students and alumni can curate more intimate connections based on interests outside the professional realm—allowing students to reach out before they begin looking for a job.

How it works

Students first put their interests into the platform. They are free to add or remove them as their interests grow during their time at Dartmouth. These seedlings of interest grow into their own personal DartTree.


Each branch of the DartTree is a different interest. A number corresponds to each branch that indicates the number of alumni in the system with the same interest.


Clicking on a branch allows the student to access the alumni who share a similar interest.


Clicking on an alum gives the student more information. DartTree provides the alum's contact information, their employment history, and other information such as a personal website. Most importantly, alumni are required to provide a profile picture. These elements give each alum's profile more of a personal feel rather than the existing ACN's catalogue-style database.


Features


Centralization
One big problem with existing resources that is many students cannot locate them. Because they are so hard to find, some students don't even know that they exist. One simple solution would be to link Dartmouth's career site (Dartboard) on the student gateway page (Darthub) so that students have a centralized entry point to any resource they might need.

Alumni on campus
When we talked to the assistant director of the Center for Professional Develpment she told us that the Hanover Inn has an alumni board that displays all alumni that are on campus each day/week. Most students are unaware of this and the board itself is out of the way from where students would ordinarily venture. So, we thought to digitize this board for easier access. If an alum was on campus, the tower icon next to their name would light up, allowing students the chance to connect with them in person.


Pet peeves and networking preferences
Another barrier students face when reaching out to alumni is the fear of "cold-calling." Students agonize over sending the perfect email—attempting to strike the perfect balance between professionalism and sounding human. By allowng alumni to add their pet peeves and networking preferences, we hope to reduce friction in reaching out to someone for the first time.
