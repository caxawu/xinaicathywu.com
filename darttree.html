The Design Process
3 Weeks





DartTree is a redesign of the Dartmouth Alumni Career Network that connects people based on interests both inside and outside of the professional realm. It also allows students to connect more easily and more comfortably with alumni by making profiles a better reflection of who a person is.

Full case study




 The project brief

Authentic design develops from deeply understanding the real needs of end users. Address your team's specific challenge of improving the experience of Dartmouth students.

Use the design process to enable students to prepare for and pursue careers "off the beaten path."




 The defining

To tackle this challenge, my team first set out to understand the scope of this task and specifically what it means for a career to be "off the beaten path."


This meant that before we started designing, we had to clarify some assumptions. We all assumed that finance and consulting career paths were the most popular and that this sector would make up about half of the graduating student population. Instead of proceeding with our assumptions though, we wanted to proceed with real data. So, we sorted through statistics released by Dartmouth and found Dartmouth's 2018 Cap & Gown Survey.




From the data that this survey provided, it became rather clear what the distribution of career paths were. In the words of Mindy Kaling:



We found that students intending to get a job in finance or consulting made up 47% of the graduating student population. However, we also found that the data also showed that 16% of the graduating population are going into the tech industry. Because this was a significant number of students, we decided that tech jobs would also be considered "on the beaten path."





 The user

Now that we had clarified our assumptions and better understood the task at hand, we formally defined our user group:

The 37% of students who were pursuing jobs in fields other than finance, consulting, or tech.






 The interviews

In order to gain a variety of perspectives, we conducted interviews to gain insights into how students were currently using resources to help them pursue their interests and careers.

We interviewed a total of 20 interviews that covered:
⬡ People on "the beaten path" & people off "the beaten path."
⬡ Dartmouth students who were seeking jobs & Dartmouth alums who were already employed.
⬡ Students from all current class years & recent graduates.




 The analysis

During our interviews, we asked our interviewees about their general experiences with finding a career path. We also asked them about their experiences with Dartmouth's Center for Professional Development, or the CPD, as it is Dartmouth's primary resource for career help. We consolidated our data and pulled out insights that would help inform our designs.





This timeline helped us visualize our interviewees' experiences with CPD in terms of their year and if they were on or off "the beaten path." We found that though students' years didn't matter much, almost all the interviewees who were "on the beaten path" made use of the CPD while almost all of those who were "off the beaten path" did not.


To find out why this pattern existed, we looked at quotes from the interviewees:


Of the students who were considered "off the beaten path, two quotes (in the green) especially stood out to us." The sentiment that "The alumni network is so diverse, but the CPD offerings just aren't." was echoed by many interviewees. Similarly, there was a general view that the CPD was only helpful to those going into finance or consulting. These views were largely due to the fact that students "off the beaten path" felt like the CPD didn't know how to help or have the resources to help—as the CPD does not have as many connections to more niche career paths. Another finding was that interviewees who were on sports teams had a strong support group from both classmates and from alumni as they had a shared common interest.

⬡ Insight: Students "off the beaten path" found alumni to be the most helpful.

⬡ Insight: Students had success in connecting with people of similar interests.



To summarize these findings, we found that what students "on the beaten path" and athletes had (and what students "off the beaten path" lacked) was support—specifically the support from others with similar interests and/or on similar paths.

 The product


DartTree is an interest-based network that connects alumni and students through information that is not found on Dartmouth Alumni Career Network.



DartTree focuses on shared interests between students and alumni outside of just the professional realm. This creates a strong connection that is similar to that of students and alumni from athletic teams. Because connections can be created through any student involvement on campus, students can curate more intimate relationships with alumni sooner.



Each branch of a student's DartTree represents a different interest. The number under each branch is the number of alumni with that same interest.



This tree is interactive such that if the student zooms in on a part of their DartTree, they have access to the alumni who share one or more of the student's interests.



Zooming in on one alum reveals more information. DartTree provides the alum's contact information, his/her employment history, and other information such as a personal website. These aspects allow the alum's profile to have a more personal feel than the existing Dartmouth Alumni Career Networks catalogue-style database. DartTree would also require a profile picture and inputting interests so that students immediately feel a more personal connection to the alum.

This redesign targets the awkward nature of contacting and interacting with alumni without knowing anything about them outside of the professional world.





 The features


Outside of the main uses of DartTree, we thought of a few ways to improve the experience of connecting with alumni.



Centralization
One problem with the existing resources is that many students cannot locate them. Because they are so hard to find, some students don't even know that they exist. One simple solution would be to link Dartmouth's career site (Dartboard) on the student gateway page (Darthub), so that students have a centralized entry point to all resources available to them.




Alumni on Campus
When we talked to the assistant director of the Center for Professional Development, she told us that the Hanover Inn has an alumni board that displays all alumni who are on campus each week. Most students are unaware of this and the board is in a location where students rarely pass by. As part of DartTree, we want to digitize the board for easier access.

One page in DartTree would have all the alumni who are on campus.




Each alum would also have a bell tower icon next to their profile picture that lights up when he/she is on campus.


Pet Peeves & Networking Preferences
Another barrier students face when reaching out to alumni is the fear of "cold-calling." Students agonize over sending the perfect email—attempting to strike the perfect balance between professionalism and sounding human. By allowing alumni to add their pet peeves and networking preferences, we hope to reduce friction in reaching out to someone for the first time.








 The takeaways


This project taught me a lot about designing real-world solutions to a very immediate problem.

I found it to be rewarding that we followed the design process all the way through and found a solution to a problem that has not yet been solved. Thoughout this process, I also learned and understood a lot more about this problem that students face. I had the chance to deeply understand the need of a very real user group, since the job search is such an important process that virtually everyone has to go through. Ultimately, this project was definitely a challenge, but I loved being able to apply my design skills to a real-world problem.
