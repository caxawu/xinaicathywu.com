Tarsier Goggles is an educational tool for students to learn interactively in the classroom. It enables users to experience the world from the perspective of a tarsier and focuses on the visual system—allowing students to see and learn firsthand the differences between animal visions.

 The team






 The background

Tarsiers are a small primate that live in the Borneo rainforest. They are nocturnal, dichromatic (red-green colorblind) and carnivorous. What makes them special is that they have the largest eyes-to-body ratio out of all mammals. Their brain-sized eyes maximizes their ability to see in low-light to better hunt prey.







 The goal


The goal of Tarsier Goggles is to provide an educational experience, primarily for students, but also for anyone interested in learning about how different animals see. As a member of the DALI lab, we partnered with the Dartmouth Center for the Advancement of Learning (DCAL) and two biology professors. This project has been in the works for a few terms, but our main goal was to bring this project to Dartmouth classrooms and labs.

Our task this term was to:

 1. Migrate the project from the HTC Vive to the more portable Oculus Quest so that the program could be used in classrooms without the need for wires, sensors, high powered computers, or expensive headsets.
2. Optimize assets, scripts, and optics features so that the program could run on the Oculus Quest without lag as the HTC Vive was a more powerful headset.
3. Improve usability and intuitiveness so that anyone can put on the headset and learn about vision.
4. Add a new tutorial system so that first-time VR users can learn the basics of controls and be guided through the experience with minimal to no outside guidance.
5. Add interactions with katydids and redesign the scenes to make the experience more immersive.




 The user


Tarsier Goggles was designed for students in Dartmouth’s Biology 14 class set to run next term. However, multiple departments across campus, from the Anthropology department to the Dartmouth medical school to even an English class, are interested in incorporating this project into their courses.





 The app


The program consists of four scenes—each designed for a specific purpose.




The lobby

The user learns the basics of how to use the controllers as they get used to VR.



The labyrinth

The user learns about differences in brightness between human and tarsier vision as they try to navigate to the center of a dark labyrinth.



The matrix

The user learns about the color differences between human and tarsier vision as we simulate a dichromat’s vision.



The borneo forest

The user sees from a tarsier’s eyes in the natural habitat of the tarsiers. They can hop from sapling to sapling and try to catch katydids. The katydids’ sound also changes as the user switches between human and tarsier as tarsiers would be better at localizing the bugs than humans. The user can also better understand how tarsiers look around. (Since their eyes are so big, their eyes can't rotate so they move their whole head instead.) To simulate this, tarsier vision appears blurry until the user moves their head to a specific object and that sole object comes into focus.

 The user test


We user tested Tarsier Goggles with BIO 14 TA’s, professors, and first-time VR users.










 The iteration


We found that first-time users of VR especially relied heavily on us guiding them through the experience despite there being an in-app tutorial. So, we redesigned the tutorial panel multiple times so that the wording was clear and was as helpful as possible. Each iteration of user testing gave us feedback on the usability and intuitiveness of the program which we used to refine the experience.

Ultimately, we implemented a new tutorial system, redesigned the entire forest scene, and refined the details of the tarsier visual system to be as accurate as possible.







 The challenges


One of the biggest challenges we faced throughout the whole process was teaching people how to use a VR headset for the first time as well as teaching them about the tarsier visual system.


We found that even though we had a tutorial, people were not reading it because the colors made it hard to read and the words were just slightly too close to their headset. So, we did a lot of iterating on the tutorial to maximize its usefulness.
Another challenge was optimization. Because the Oculus Quest has less processing power than the HTC Vive, we had to redesign all the scenes and incorporate all the new functions without lag. We spent a lot of time reducing poly counts of our 3D models, simplifying all the scenes, and editing lighting. While these definitely helped, we had to keep this limitation in mind throughout every step of the process.





 The takeaways


This was a really exciting project to work on as this project is leading the initiative to incorporate VR into classrooms. I really enjoyed working on something that has the potential to be so impactful for our fellow classmates. This project also taught me a lot about how VR can be used in an educational space where it has the ability to teach people first-hand something that they would otherwise read about and memorize from a textbook.


I learned a lot from working as a team, collaborating with our partners, and applying my knowledge to develop this project.
