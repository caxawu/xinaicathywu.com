Smartphone Lending is a research project centered around exploring how people share technology. We focused on a pain point within that experience where people are uncomfortable with letting another person borrow their phone. Using the reseach, I prototyped an app that allows people to quickly "lock" their phone in certain modes with restricted access to apps before letting someone borrow it.

Research case study




 The project


As a project in Dartmouth's HCI lab, the Smartphone Lending project focused on how human-to-human connection could be facilitated by technology. My role was to research this interaction and build an app that improves the way people interact with each other during phone-lending situations.



 The research

To target this problem, we first looked at the current state of this interaction. The goal of this research phase was to investigate the experience of phone-lending to inform design considerations in a phone-lending interaction. We interviewed 21 participants and found several patterns in the data.



Lending habits by group

During the interviews, we asked participants about their experiences with lending their phone. The graphs are a synthesis of the habits that they mentioned. Because we did not prompt participants about any specific groups, participants generated categories of people themselves. However, some participants did not mention some of the categories (marked in gray).


People were more willing to hand their unlocked phone to a family member or friend without first taking any precautions or asking for a reason
(marked in dark blue).

  

The strangers category is the only place where we see that people will deny all access.


Concerns about phone lending

We asked the participants about the reasons why they woud not lend their phones. We did not prompt the participants, so all of these reasons were generated by the participants. But, there may be more concerns that participants may not have thought of or may not have told us. Regardless, this list indicates the concerns that were at the front of their minds.


The top concern was borrowers seeing text messages.

 Interestingly, lenders were most concerned about the privacy of their contacts. People were worried about a borrower seeing a sensitive text chain as it compromises the privacy of the person at the other end of the texts.

 

The second-most prominent concern was theft.

 This concern typically only applied to strangers. Several participants also said that they were likely to judge a borrower's appearance and intentions to determine trustworthiness before lending their phone.











 The user




After exploring the current state of smartphone-lending situations, I had a better sense of our user. I found that in the current state of phone-sharing, most people are either unwilling or uncomfortable handing their device over to someone who asks to borrow it. However, since people are generally nice, they allow others to borrow their phone even though they are worried about others seeing sensitive content.

So, I came to the conclusion that our users are smartphone owners who don't want to say no to a borrower, but are not comfortable with allowing them full access of their phone.

 The prototype


This smartphone-lending app “locks” the phone in certain preset modes (strangers mode, friends mode, etc). The modes restrict access of the phone until the owner unlocks their phone using their typical unlocking method such as fingerprint, faceID, or password.








Presets


In our research, we found that people typically categorized borrowers into four categories. Because these categories were the same among most people, I decided to make them into existing presets.


The app comes preloaded with the four preset modes:
1. Strangers
2. Acquaintances
3. Friends
4. Family


Users have the ability to modify these presets according to their own preferences. They can also add their own preset mode. By default, the family preset is the least restrictive while the strangers preset is the most restrictive.






Security options


Within each mode, the user can choose which apps they wish to grant/restrict access to. Each app can be configured to have one of three security options.


1. Hide app: The app is not present on the home page.








With this on, the lender protects their phone by making the borrower think that the app is not downloaded at all.




2. Dummy app: The app is present, but is not functional.








When the app is clicked, the borrower sees an error such as a network connectivity error instead of seeing the lender's information.




3. Privacy lock: The app is functional, but has features restricted.








This mode allows the borrower to use the app, but certain features like search history are restricted.




Launch options


A user can launch their preset in multiple ways:


Fingerprint scanner
Using the scanner on the back of a smartphone allows for a total of 9 max presets (with the 10th finger to unlock the phone normally).

Smartwatch
Clicking our app icon and using the side dial to scroll through presets allows for any number of presets.

Quick settings dropdown menu
Each preset mode can be configured to an icon that can be added to the pulldown settings menu where users access things like the flashlight and WiFi settings.









Granting access


I also found that as granting access is as important as restricting it.


Imagine an interaction where:
1. A borrower needs a phone to call a friend, but realizes that he/she does not have
    the person's contact number.
2. The borrower tries to access Facebook, but is denied access.
3. The lender sees that the borrower needs to contact the friend and decides to let
    the borrower use Facebook.
4. The borrower hands the phone back to the lender.
5. The lender exits out of our app to allow normal functionalities.
6. The lender hands the phone back to the borrower.


This kind of interaction is awkward and uncomfortable. Most importantly though, it becomes unsafe as the borrower now has a completely unlocked phone and can now access everything. To target this issue, I needed a way to grant access to a specific app.

So, I added a quick access feature where people can add any apps they may want to grant access to depending on a situation. This feature is most beneficial on a smartwatch as lenders can quickly scroll through their quick access app list.






When the situation arises, lenders can click the quick access icon on their smartwatch and use the side dial to scroll through their list and allow functionalities of a specific app.



Wouldn't people be offended if they had restricted access?

Because the methods of launching are discreet, most of the time, borrowers wouldn't even know that they have restricted access. They would likely think that there is really a connection error or that the lender just does not have a certain app installed.

Further, our research showed that people only ask to borrow a phone when they are really in need. Therefore, their need for a phone would generally outweigh any offense they might take from the lender appearing to not trust them by restricting access.


What happens when people learn the "tricks" of launching the modes?

Even if people learned the common features of the app and knew they had restricted access, the lender could reframe the interaction to ensure that there is no awkwardness. Instead of framing the interaction as one of distrust, the lender could easily explain that they use one mode when lending to anyone regardless of it that is true or not.










 The bonus features




Another big reason people don’t lend their phones is due to concerns of theft. The “other security” option targets this concern.

Anti-theft

If the phone is on a mode with anti-theft enabled, the phone’s accelerometer turns on once that mode launches. It can be customized to run actions like blaring an alarm and locking the sim card tray so the phone remains traceable. When the phone's accelerometer detects running, these actions launch.

Anti-pickpocket

If the phone is on a mode with anti-pickpocket enabled, the phone "locks" in this mode. The phone will not turn on or function at all until the phone owner normally unlocks their phone. Since many pickpockets are discreet and slip away, using the accelerometer is not effective. Instead, a phone on this mode can be paired with any secondary device that has a bluetooth connection (like a smartwatch or wireless earbuds). If a phone leaves a certain range with the secondary device, it can initiate a series of actions like sounding an alarm and locking the sim card tray.














The takeaways




This project gave me the chance to think about how people interact with each other about technology.
I learned a lot about people's relationship with their devices and with each other. I realized that because people tend to be non-confrontational (at least among the people we interviewed), they lack a subtle way to say no to others. By prototyping an app like this, I was able to really focus in on a specific type of user and how to improve their experiences with each other that are facilitated by technology.
