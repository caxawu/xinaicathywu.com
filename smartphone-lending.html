Smartphone Lending

UX Researcher | UI/UX Designer

Smartphone Lending is a research project centered around exploring how people share technology. Because phones contain so much personal information, people are uncomfortable with lending their phone to others. So, I prototyped a security app that allows people to quickly and discreetly "lock" their phone in modes with restricted access before letting someone borrow it.

The background
As a project in the XDiscovery HCI lab, the Smartphone Lending project was focused on how human-to-human connection could be facilitated by technology. Specifically, we were interested in the moment of interaction when someone asks to borrow someone else’s phone. The goal was to investigate this interaction to help inform design considerations that might improve this experience.
The research
We began by conducting interviews with 21 participants who were all Dartmouth undergrads. The interview was divided into two parts:

1. An experiment: We interviewed all the participants in pairs so one of us could meet the participant at the scheduled time and ask to borrow their phone to contact our partner who was “late.” We used this to directly observe the phone-lending behavior of our participants.
2. The actual interview: We then sat down with the participant and asked them questions about their experiences with lending their phone.
Key takeaways
Lending habits by group
When we asked participants about their phone-lending experiences, most people broke down their experiences in terms of these four groups. This graph represents how the participants’ behavior differs when lending to each group.
The main insights were:
  • People were more willing to hand their unlocked phone to a family member or friend without first taking any precautions or asking for a reason (dark blue).
  • The strangers category is the only place where people will refuse to lend their phone (black).


    • Concerns about phone-lending
    We also asked participants to list the reasons why they might be hesitant to lend their phone.
    The main insights were:
  • The #1 concern was message history.
    Interestingly, participants were not concerned about borrowers seeing the messages that they sent out to other people. Rather, they were worried about borrowers seeing a sensitive text chain and compromises the privacy of the person at the other end of the texts.
  • The #2 concern was theft.
    This concern only applied when lending to strangers. Several participants said they first would determine the stranger’s trustworthiness by judging their appearance and intentions before lending their phone.






    • Research conclusions
    Design considerations
    Based on the insights from our study, we outlined guidelines for possible solutions that might improve this phone lending/borrowing interaction.
    The three main guidelines were:
    Customizability: The solution should allow for varying levels of restrictions and have a one-time setup.
    Inconspicuousness: The borrower should not notice that the lender has set restrictions specific to the borrower. The solution should have a quick and discreet activation.
    Theft and privacy: Our features should make the lender more comfortable and confident in lending their phone.


    Proposed solutions
    With the guidelines in mind, we thought about how to create a solution to facilitate easier phone lending/borrowing interactions. To target all three guidelines, we proposed an app where users could customize profiles with varying levels of restrictions that could be launched quickly and discreetly so that they could feel safer when sharing their phones.
    Outside of the core functionality, we also ideated on other features that would work in conjunction:
    Smartwatch-enabled: The idea behind this feature is to allow the owner of the phone to essentially “keep tabs” on what a borrower is doing on their phone. The smartwatch screen could display exactly what the borrower is seeing and could allow the owner to remotely lock certain apps or lock the entire phone. This feature also highlights customizability as users can change the level of restriction on their phone as someone is using it.
    Fingerprint scanner: Many smartphones have a fingerprint scanner and allow for multiple prints to be saved. Most people only use a few fingers to unlock their phones—so, we decided to use this to our advantage. This feature proposes using the fingerprint scanner to launch the profiles that users have created. By using “uncommon” fingers like the fourth finger or the pinky, users can quickly and inconspicuously launch a mode without the awkwardness of a borrower ever realizing that they have restricted access.
    Hardware protection: Because one of the top concerns in phone-lending experiences is the fear of theft, we decided to implement a hardware protection element. With this element on, if the phone’s accelerometer detects running (oh no a thief!), the phone would activate an alarm alerting everyone nearby. (“This person is holding a stolen phone! Stop them!”) In addition, this trigger could also lock the sim card tray—preventing the thief from rendering the phone untraceable.
    The definition
    After the research phase, this project was technically over. But, rather than just leaving the project with the research and proposed solutions, I wanted to use the research to actually build a solution. So, I ventured on as a one-person team. But, before jumping into design, I wanted to define some things more formally to guide me through the rest of the design process.
    The user
    During the research phase, we all had a general idea of who the user was. But, rather than just running with a rather vague definition of “smartphone owners in a phone-lending situation,” I wanted a better sense of exactly who I would be designing for. Looking back on the research, I found a trend where many people are unwilling or uncomfortable with their device in someone else’s hands. However, people are generally nice (as least true for the people we interviewed). Because of that, they were willing to let others borrow their phone despite their discomfort in order to avoid confrontations and not say no.


    So, I formally defined users as: Smartphone owner who are uncomfortable with allowing others full access of their phone, but are too nice to say no.

    The problem
    I wanted to better define the problem as well. Pinpointing the user helped me to pull out the main problem that I would be tackling: helping a phone-lender feel more comfortable with their device in someone else’s hands.


    The ideation
    Now that I had a clearly defined direction, I could think more critically about the solution. The proposed solution from our research phase (an app that allows users to customize profiles with varying levels of restrictions) was a great starting point—my research team and I had decided on it it during a ideation session towards the end of our research. Because the proposed solution was guided by research, I decided to run with it. I kept the core functionalities and features and decided to build onto the basic structure. So, I set about fleshing out the details of how a user might interact with this app.




    Conclusions and realizations
    After thinking more critically about this app, I realized that it was really easy to make the app overly complicated with features and customizations. I knew that most of all, I wanted to focus on create an experience where the interaction between the user and the app was simple and intuitive. Guided by this thought, I split the app into the three most important components:


    Presets
    I noticed that I had to balance customability and presets to accomodate all kinds of users. Because our research showed that many people naturally talked about people in terms of four main groups (family, friends, acquaintances, and strangers), I decided to build those four presets into the app. This is especially useful for users who don’t want to spend much time on customizations and just want general functionalities to protect their device. Rather than just limiting it to just four presets, I thought to allow users to add their own preset for power users who enjoy customizing every little detail.

    Launching
    I also came up with multiple ideas of how to launch the modes. This included using the fingerprint scanner that my research team and I had originally thought of, but also included potentially using a smartwatch or phones’ pulldown settings menu. Ultimately, I decided to include all three as ways to launch modes. This decision was driven by trying to make the app accessible to a range of smartphone users such as those who might have a broken fingerprint scanner or those who have a phone without a fingerprint scanner at all.

    Granting access
    While I was thinking about how a user could customize a preset for a certain lending situation, I realized that granting access to an app is as important as restricting access.

    Consider this interaction between a lender and a borrower:
    1. The borrower asks the lender for their phone to call a friend, but they don’t remember their friend’s number.
    2. The borrower asks the lender if they can use Facebook instead, but Facebook is restricted.
    3. The lender decides to allow Facebook access so they ask for their phone back.
    4. The lender exits our app to allow normal functionalities and hands the phone back to the borrower.

    This kind of interaction is rather awkward. More importantly, it defeats the purpose of a security app as the borrower is now holding a completely unlocked phone and has access to everything. I needed a way to allow for granting access after the preset mode had been launched. I decided that this could be accomplished through a quick access feature where people can add apps they may want to change access to depending on the situation. Though optional, this feature would be most beneficial with a secondary device like a a smartwatch where the lender could quickly scroll through their quick access app list.

    User flow
    Even with the research distilled into three main components, it was still hard for me to visualize how this app would function in action during a lending situation. So, being a very visual person, I created a user flow to see all the paths that a lending situation could take. This flow was really helpful in helped me keep the big picture in mind and make sure that what I was making would fit well into this flow.
    The UI
    All that work finally brought me to the UI. I knew what features I wanted to include, so I started thinking about the flow between screens. Because my goal was to make the interactionswith the app simple and intuitive, I focused on making sure that the navigation really clear and that the app didn’t have much of a learning curve to it.




    Sketching helped me to think through the app on paper. Once I had a good idea of the basic flow and elements, I moved to Figma for grayscales as I tried out different layouts.



    Each iteration informed the next to get me to the final hi-fi mockups. As I iterated, I prioritized a simplicity—both visually and navigationally.

    The prototype
    The smartphone-lending app “locks” the phone in certain preset modes (strangers mode, friends mode, etc). The modes restrict access to predetermined apps so the phone owner feels more comfortable with their phone in a borrower’s hands. The phone stays in the restricted mode until the owner unlocks their phone using their typical unlocking method such as fingerprint,faceID, or password.


    Presets
    The app comes preloaded with the four preset modes:

    1. Strangers (most restrictive)
    2. Acquaintances
    3. Friends
    4. Family (least restrictive)


    Because our interviewees naturally grouped borrowers into these four categories, I listed these four as the defaults under the presets section of the home page. This allows for easy access for users who might not want to customize their presets.

    For users who do enjoy customization, the “Add Preset” button allows them to create an entirely new category and add various levels of restrictions to their heart’s content.


    Security options

    Within each preset, the user can choose which apps they wish to restrict access to. Each app can be configured to have one of three security options.


    1. Hide app: The app is not present on the home page.

    Under this option, the lender protects their phone by making the borrower think that the app is not downloaded at all.




    2. Dummy app: The app is present, but not functional.

    Upon clicking the app, the borrower sees a “fake” error such as a network connectivity error. This prevents the borrower from accessing the app’s functionality.




    3. Privacy lock: The app is functional, but has features restricted.

    The borrower can click into and use the app. But, certain features like posting functionality and search history are restricted.






    Launch options
    The next section on the home page is the launch options—how to actually trigger the presets and “lock” the phone.

    I had a lot of different launch options to consider—from secondary device such as wireless airbuds and powerbanks to a button in the app itself. Ultimately, my decision was made with accessibility in mind as these three options reach a range of smartphone users.
    Smartwatch
    Clicking our app icon and using the side dial to scroll through presets allows for any number of presets.

    Fingerprint scanner
    Using the scanner on the back of a smartphone allows for a total of 9 max presets (with the 10th finger to unlock the phone normally).

    Settings dropdown menu
    Each preset mode can be configured to an icon that can be added to the pulldown settings menu where users access things like the flashlight and WiFi settings.




    Granting access
    Because I also found that as granting access is as important as restricting it, I thought to add it as its own section on the home page.

    In the case where the lender (the owner of the smartphone) wants to allow the borrower access to a restricted app, this section lets them to quickly grant access without needing to exit the preset.

    In this section, users can see at a glance all of the quick access apps. Users can easily add apps to the list in order to allow for more flexibility in a phone-lending situation.

    This feature is most beneficial on a device such as a smartwatch as lenders can use the dial on the side of their watch to quickly scroll through their quick access app list and grant access without needing to take back their smartphone first.




    Hardware concerns
    To target people’s second-most prominent concern of theft, the “other security options” make people feel more comfortable lending their phones.

    Anti-theft
    If anti-theft is enabled, the phone’s accelerometer turns on once the preset launches. If the accelerometer detects running, custom actions such as blaring an alarm and locking the sim card tray launch.

    Anti-pickpocket
    Since many pickpockets are discreet and slip away, using the accelerometer is not effective. If anti-pickpocket is enabled, the phone will pair with any secondary device that has a bluetooth connection (like a smartwatch or wireless earbuds). If the phone leaves the range of the bluetooth connection, it initiate a series of actions such as sounding an alarm and locking the sim card tray.
    The takeaways
    This project made me really think about how to design human interactions that are facilitated by technology. I learned a lot about people's relationship with their devices and with each other. This was also my first design project that had hardware integrations. Needing to consider how hardware like smartwatches and smartphones fit into a phone-lending situation made me really focus on designing the interaction between the lender and the borrower rather than on the interface of the app itself.

    This project also really taught me the merits of conducting good research during the early stages.
    I could clearly see the benefits of the research informing design decisions as it made the process of designing much easier. This was especially beneficial when I was designing alone because I could lean on the research through the design process.
